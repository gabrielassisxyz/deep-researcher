# Beyond the Commit (BNY Mellon / ICSE SEIP 2026)

Independent academic + industry (BNY Mellon). Accepted at ICSE SEIP. Large survey +
interviews. Added in Round 3 after the review panel flagged it as a deferred-but-unread
lead.

## Setup

- 2,989 developer survey responses + 11 in-depth interviews at BNY Mellon.
- Mixed-methods: validity of different approaches to evaluating AI productivity impact.
- Venue: ICSE SEIP (peer-reviewed software engineering track).

## Key findings

- "Survey results expose **conflicting perspectives on AI tool usefulness**" — within the
  same org, developers disagree on whether AI helps.
- Interviews elicit **six distinct factors** capturing both short-term and long-term
  productivity dimensions.
- **"In contrast to prior work, our factors highlight the importance of long-term metrics
  like technical expertise and ownership of work."** — i.e. short-term speed metrics miss
  long-term costs the developers themselves flag.
- Advocates multifaceted/human-centered measurement over commit-based or speed-only
  metrics.

## Why this matters for the dossier

- Directly corroborates the measured-vs-self-reported divergence and the long-term-cost
  thesis (Q5/Q6) from a third independent angle: a large enterprise survey finds
  developers' perspectives on AI usefulness conflict, and long-term factors (expertise,
  ownership) are undercounted by short-term metrics.
- Independent + peer-reviewed + 2026 → strengthens the recency of the "non-speed /
  long-term costs" axis.
- Caveat: survey + interviews (self-report + qualitative), not an RCT; gives perception +
  framework, not measured speedup.

## Claim typing

- Fact (study exists, methods): high.
- Performance (developers' perspectives on AI usefulness conflict; long-term factors
  matter): medium — large survey, peer-reviewed, but self-report/qualitative.
