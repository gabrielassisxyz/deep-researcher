# Cui et al. High-Skilled Work Field Experiments

## Source Type

Working paper from MIT Economics using randomized field experiments at Microsoft, Accenture, and an anonymous Fortune 100 company. Independence is limited because Microsoft/GitHub/Accenture data and employees were involved, but the methodology is materially stronger than self-report.

## Method

Three large-scale randomized controlled trials in real workplace environments. A random subset of 4,867 software developers received access to GitHub Copilot. Outcomes included completed tasks, commits, build counts, and build success rate.

## Key Evidence

- The pooled analysis estimates a 26.08% increase in completed tasks among developers using the AI tool, with standard error 10.3%.
- Secondary outcomes are directionally consistent: 13.55% more code updates and 38.38% more compilations.
- Less-experienced developers had higher adoption rates and greater productivity gains.
- For Microsoft, the paper reports Copilot significantly raised task completion for more recent hires and junior positions, but not for developers with longer tenure or more senior positions.
- The authors highlight noisy experiments, imperfect compliance, short treatment windows, and large variation in output.

## Caveats

The main outcome is completed tasks or PR-like workflow units, not direct elapsed time on comparable tasks. The tool is Copilot-style autocomplete rather than newer autonomous agents. Results are stronger for junior or recent-hire populations than for senior developers.

## Practical Implication

This is the strongest positive measured evidence for professional developers, but it does not support a blanket claim that experienced senior developers in familiar codebases become faster.
