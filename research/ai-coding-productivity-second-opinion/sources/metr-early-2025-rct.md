# METR Early-2025 RCT

## Source Type

Independent nonprofit research, released as an arXiv preprint and METR blog post. Not peer-reviewed in the arXiv record.

## Method

Randomized controlled trial with 16 experienced open-source developers completing 246 real tasks in mature repositories where they had an average of about five years of prior experience. Issues were randomized to allow or disallow AI. AI-allowed work primarily used Cursor Pro with Claude 3.5/3.7 Sonnet.

## Key Evidence

- The study directly targets the strict user question: experienced developers, real tasks, and familiar repositories.
- AI-allowed tasks took 19% longer than non-AI tasks.
- Developers forecast a 24% speedup before tasks and still estimated a 20% speedup afterward.
- METR explicitly warns against over-generalizing to all software developers or all settings.
- The result is now marked historically important but partly out of date by METR because later agentic tools and usage patterns changed during 2025.

## Caveats

The sample is small, though task count is larger. Participants are open-source developers, not necessarily representative of company teams. The tool mix is early-2025 Cursor and Claude, not all current coding agents. Timing depended on developer self-reported implementation time, though tasks were screen recorded.

## Practical Implication

This is the strongest direct evidence against the popular general claim. It shows that, in at least one realistic expert/familiar-codebase setting, measured speed and perceived speed diverged sharply.
