# Do AI coding agents actually make experienced developers faster?

## The decision this has to support

I build software with AI coding agents every day, and I have reorganised my whole workflow
around them. Before I go further down that road — and before I recommend it to anyone else —
I want to know whether the productivity gain is **real**, and under what conditions.

Not "is AI useful". **Does it make an experienced developer measurably faster on real work in
a codebase they already know?**

## What makes this question hard, and why I want it researched rather than answered

Almost everyone with an answer is selling something. Vendors publish their own numbers. So do
the companies whose valuations depend on those numbers being true. And developers report their
own experience, which is not the same as measuring it.

**A vendor is a primary source about its own pricing and a marketer about its own results.**
Treat every performance claim accordingly, whoever makes it — including claims that agree with
me.

## Deliberately not telling you

- **No candidate studies, no starting URLs, no papers.** Finding what evidence exists is half
  the research. If the only studies in the dossier are ones I could have named, that is
  retrieval, not research.
- **No hypothesis I want confirmed.** If the honest answer is "the gains are smaller than
  claimed", or "they are real but only in these conditions", or "the evidence does not
  support a general answer", say that.

Look specifically for evidence that **contradicts** the popular claim, and for evidence that
**self-reported** speedup diverges from **measured** speedup. If those diverge, that divergence
is the most important finding in the dossier.

## Questions I want answered

- What controlled or measured evidence exists — not surveys of how people *feel*?
- Does the effect differ by **task type** (greenfield vs. existing codebase, boilerplate vs.
  novel logic, well-specified vs. ambiguous)?
- Does it differ by **developer experience level**? By **familiarity with the codebase**?
- Where the evidence conflicts, *why* does it conflict? Different methods, populations, tasks?
- What is the effect on things that are not speed — defect rates, review burden, comprehension
  of the resulting code, maintenance cost?

## Required outputs

- The evidence you **found**, and the evidence you **looked for and could not find** — the
  second list matters as much as the first.
- Sources typed honestly: vendor-authored, independent, self-reported, peer-reviewed.
- A decision guide: under what conditions the gain is supported by evidence, and under what
  conditions it is not.
- **What the web cannot settle**, named as such — with the local evaluation that would settle
  it, concretely enough that I could run it.

## How I will judge this dossier

By its audit trail, not by whether I like the conclusion.

- Is the search ledger real — including the queries that found nothing?
- Did discovery surface evidence I did not point you at?
- Did every high-impact gap reach `resolved` or `blocked`, with no `partially resolved`
  covering an early exit?
- Is any performance claim resting on a vendor's own publication at `high` confidence?
- Is the fit question — *"does this make **me** faster"* — named as unanswerable from web
  evidence, rather than quietly answered?
